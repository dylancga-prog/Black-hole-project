Sources

This project uses three primary sources spanning over 100 years of black hole research. Each source represents a direct contribution to the history covered in this timeline, from the original mathematical prediction to the first real photograph. Citations are formatted in Chicago Manual of Style.

Theory, 1916

On the Gravitational Field of a Point Mass, According to Einstein's Theory

Karl Schwarzschild, 1916
1

This is the original paper in which Karl Schwarzschild solved Einstein's field equations and mathematically described what we now call a black hole. Written while Schwarzschild was serving as an artillery officer on the Russian front during World War I, it introduced the concept of the Schwarzschild radius, the critical boundary beyond which nothing can escape a black hole's gravity. This paper is the direct theoretical origin of the concept of black holes and is the starting point of this entire project.

Schwarzschild, Karl. "Uber das Gravitationsfeld eines Massenpunktes nach der Einsteinschen Theorie." Sitzungsberichte der Preussischen Akademie der Wissenschaften, 1916. English translation available via ResearchGate.

Discovery, 1972

Observation of a Celestial X-ray Source in Cygnus

B.L. Webster and P. Murdin, Nature, 1972
2

This paper by astronomers B.L. Webster and P. Murdin provided the first strong observational evidence that black holes actually exist in nature. By measuring the orbital motion of a companion star in the Cygnus constellation, they calculated that the invisible object it orbited was far too massive to be any known type of star or compact object. Their conclusion was that it had to be a black hole, the first time that claim was supported by real observational data. This paper represents the bridge between theory and physical reality in this project.

Webster, B.L., and P. Murdin. "Cygnus X-1: a Spectroscopic Binary with a Heavy Companion?" Nature 235, no. 5332 (1972): 37-38.

First image, 2019

First M87 Event Horizon Telescope Results. I. The Shadow of the Supermassive Black Hole

Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration, The Astrophysical Journal Letters, 2019
3

This is the official scientific publication from the Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration announcing the first-ever photograph of a black hole. The paper details how a global network of eight radio observatories worked together to image the shadow of M87*, a supermassive black hole 55 million light-years away with a mass 6.5 billion times that of our Sun. The image confirmed predictions made by Einstein's General Relativity over 100 years earlier. This source closes the loop on the entire history covered in this project, from Schwarzschild's equations in 1916 to a real photograph in 2019.

Event Horizon Telescope Collaboration. "First M87 Event Horizon Telescope Results. I. The Shadow of the Supermassive Black Hole." The Astrophysical Journal Letters 875, no. 1 (2019): L1. https://doi.org/10.3847/2041-8213/ab0ec7.
